Sculpture Exhibition on Chiefdom

Africa's rich cultural, intellectual, and social discourse makes it appealing and wealthy to embrace. Chiefdom as a central nerve of African culture has informed the youth about the need to orient themselves with their culture and possibly come to terms with the background, character, problems, ideas, and way of life. Samuel Adentwi Bentum (PhD) sees the African woman as one of the strongest forces within the African circle. At this exquisite exhibition saw the display of the following artful masterpiece and many more others such as Ekumfohen(Chief Executioner), Nkyenhen (Chief Drummer), Akyeamehen (Chief Linguist) and others.

Ahemfo Nhyiam is a composition of Sub-Chiefs in a horizontal plane. In the Akan culture, Sub-Chiefs are chosen to carry out a variety of tasks on behalf of the Paramount Chief. They are consequently expected to assist the paramount chief in her duties effectively. The oval perforation and crumbled form in the upper portion are associated with day and night or the seen and unseen world. Similarly, the perforations within the mid-section and baseline signify the efforts of the Sub-Chiefs to perform in their elected offices by bringing progress and development to their community.

Mpontuhen is a highly and renowned person selected from among the youth to the status of a chief. She has the financial might and charisma to ensure the development of her community. She is satiated with wealth, ideas, thoughts and good omen that are significant for the community’s economic and social progress. Mpontuhen is not a born elite; she is not born to the royal lineage. The presence of the state mace and the acclamation by the queen mother approves of their contribution to the state and the stool of their ancestors. Hence, the mask-like facial expression of these images that signify the approval and acceptance of the unseen spirits – owners of the land.

Afonahen is a chief warrior and commander general of the paramount, or town chief. She has the overall responsibility to protect the paramountcy and her subjects from reprisals, attacks, confrontations, and takeovers from other paramount's and authorities. She has the attributes of bravery, sternness, courage, and confidence. She is the custodian of the state sword and the defense commander-in-chief of the state. She has a wreath hung around her neck as an ornament, an act that is synonymous with Akan spiritual leaders. This wreath symbolizes Afonahen’s relationship with the seen and unseen worlds, the owners of the state, and the spiritual warriors of the state in the Akan culture. Afonahen is represented in a swearing position as a symbol of her authority over her sect and service to the superior leaders of the community.
